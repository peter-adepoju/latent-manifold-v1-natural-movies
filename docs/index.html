Abstract

Natural vision drives visual cortex with rich temporal structure, but the shape of population geometry during continuous movie viewing remains difficult to characterize. Here I analyze Allen Brain Observatory V1 calcium-imaging recordings during natural movie presentation, extract real stimulus features from the movie frames, and compare low-dimensional neural representations using decoding, encoding, geometry, and brain-model alignment analyses. The strongest current result is that held-out-repeat V1 population activity predicts real movie-derived features such as RMS contrast, luminance variability, spatial-frequency centroid, orientation selectivity, and total spectral power. Latent embeddings such as PCA, UMAP, ISOMAP, and CEBRA produce distinct geometric descriptions of the same data, but their predictive power under block-wise evaluation is modest. Geometry events and trajectory dynamics are interpretable and occasionally align with visually meaningful stimulus segments, yet the evidence is descriptive rather than causal. The project is therefore best read as an honest exploratory NeuroAI study that shows where the signal is real, where it is fragile, and what additional replication is needed before broader biological claims can be defended.

Introduction

The central question is simple: when a mouse views a natural movie, does the population response in V1 occupy a structured low-dimensional manifold, and does that manifold reflect the visual content of the movie itself? The answer matters because natural stimuli are temporally autocorrelated and highly structured, which makes them both scientifically interesting and analytically dangerous.

This project sits at the intersection of computational neuroscience and NeuroAI. It is not just a dimensionality-reduction exercise. It is an attempt to connect neural activity, stimulus features, and representational geometry using public data, repeat-aware controls, and cautious interpretation.

Discussion

The project’s main lesson is nuanced rather than triumphant. V1 carries interpretable information about natural movie structure, but the most visually striking geometric descriptions are not enough on their own to support broad biological claims.

  • The strongest claim is a decoding result on real movie features.
  • Geometry is valuable, but descriptive until it generalizes robustly.
  • The current cohort is too small for broad replication claims.
  • The right next step is scale, not stronger rhetoric.
Geometry and stimulus alignment are informative, but still modest and interpretive.
